# Accounting-to-GRC Bridge Document
### Reflections on Transitioning from Financial Compliance to Governance, Risk and Compliance

## Overview
This document reflects on how a professional background 
in accounting and financial compliance has shaped my 
approach to Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC), and 
why I believe this transition is a natural progression 
rather than a complete career change.

## Internal Controls and Security Controls
In accounting, internal controls exist to prevent and 
detect errors, fraud, and misstatements within financial 
records through the separation of duties, approval 
hierarchies, and reconciliation processes. Security 
controls in cybersecurity serve a parallel purpose: 
preventing and detecting unauthorized access, misuse, 
or compromise within a system. Reviewing WAF rules, 
firewall configurations, and access settings during my 
cybersecurity internship felt conceptually familiar 
because I was already trained to ask the same underlying 
question I asked in accounting: Is this control actually 
doing what it's supposed to do, and is there evidence 
to prove it?

## Audit Mindset and Documentation Discipline
Years of preparing for and supporting financial audits 
taught me to document thoroughly, assume my work would 
be reviewed by someone else, and never rely on memory 
alone. This discipline carried directly into my security 
monitoring work, where I maintained structured daily 
observation logs covering activities, findings, analysis, 
and conclusions; the same evidentiary mindset auditors 
apply to financial statements.

## Risk Assessment and Materiality
In accounting, materiality determines which risks or 
errors are significant enough to warrant attention and 
disclosure. In GRC, a similar judgment is required to 
assess which vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, or gaps 
pose meaningful risk versus which are low priority. 
Learning to distinguish background automated traffic 
from genuine security events during my internship 
mirrored this same skill of separating noise from 
material risk.

## Compliance and Regulatory Awareness
Financial compliance work requires staying current with 
regulatory requirements and ensuring operations align 
with them. This translates directly to GRC frameworks 
such as NIST CSF 2.0 and ISO/IEC 27001, where compliance 
is similarly about aligning organizational practice with 
an established standard, and being able to demonstrate 
that alignment with evidence.

## Conclusion
Rather than starting over, I see my accounting background 
as a foundation that GRC builds upon. The core skills, 
which are control evaluation, audit documentation, risk 
judgment, and regulatory alignment, transfer directly. 
What I am developing now is the technical fluency to 
apply that same disciplined thinking to cybersecurity 
and information security environments.
